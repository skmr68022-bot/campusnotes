4.1 Contract of Sale (Meaning & Essentials)

When you walk into a shopping mall, pick up a t-shirt, pay the cashier, and walk out, you have just performed a classic "Contract of Sale". Under the Sale of Goods Act, 1930, a contract of sale is a legally binding agreement where the seller transfers or agrees to transfer the ownership of goods to the buyer for a specific money price. It is important to note that this act specifically deals with "goods" (movable property) and not immovable property like land or houses. This act is essentially the commercial backbone of India, governing every single product you buy from grocery stores, online e-commerce platforms, and wholesale markets.

For a contract of sale to be legally valid, it must meet the following essential elements under Section 4:

  • There must be at least two parties: A "Seller" and a "Buyer".
  • Subject matter must be goods: The goods must be movable and physical (excluding immovable property like land, or actionable claims like shares, which are governed by other laws).
  • There must be a price: The price must be in terms of money. It cannot be a barter system (exchanging goods for goods, which is a contract of exchange, not sale).
  • Transfer of ownership must occur: The primary objective of a sale is to transfer the proprietary rights (ownership) of the goods from the seller to the buyer.

Sale vs. Agreement to Sell (Sec 4)

Law students often confuse a "Sale" with an "Agreement to Sell". However, the distinction is absolutely crucial for your exams and for business practice. A Sale is an executed contract—it means the ownership of the goods is transferred immediately from the seller to the buyer at the time of the contract. An Agreement to Sell is an executory contract—the ownership of the goods is NOT transferred immediately; it is supposed to be transferred at a later date or upon the fulfillment of a specific condition.

Basis of Difference Sale (Sec 4(3)) Agreement to Sell (Sec 4(4))
Nature of Contract It is an Executed contract. The deal is done then and there. It is an Executory contract. The deal is promised for a future date.
Transfer of Property (Ownership) Ownership of goods passes immediately from the seller to the buyer. Ownership of goods remains with the seller; it passes to the buyer at a future date or upon a condition.
Risk of Loss Risk of loss falls on the buyer instantly. If the goods are destroyed, the buyer still has to pay the price. Risk of loss falls on the seller. If the goods are destroyed before the sale is finalized, the buyer doesn't have to pay.
Right to Sue The seller can sue the buyer for the price of the goods directly. The seller can only sue the buyer for damages for not accepting the goods, not the full price.
Examples You walk into a store, pick up a product, pay for it, and walk out. Ownership is instantly yours. You custom-order a sofa from a furniture maker; they will deliver it in 3 weeks. Ownership transfers only when they deliver it to you.

4.2 Conditions and Warranties (Sec 12-16)

When you buy something, you naturally have certain expectations. If you buy a brand new car, you expect the engine to start. If you buy a bottle of mineral water, you expect it to be sealed. In legal terms, these expectations are classified as either "Conditions" or "Warranties". Understanding the difference between the two is vital because it determines how you can react if the seller fails to meet their promise.

  • Condition (Sec 12(2)): A condition is a stipulation that is essential to the very main purpose of the contract. If a seller breaches a condition, the buyer has the drastic right to reject the goods and cancel the entire contract (rescision) as well as claim damages. For example, if you buy a tractor meant for farming, and the seller delivers a defective machine that never starts, it is a breach of condition. You can return the tractor and demand a full refund.
  • Warranty (Sec 12(3)): A warranty is a stipulation that is collateral to the main purpose of the contract. In plain English, it means it is a "side promise" rather than the core promise. If a seller breaches a warranty, the buyer cannot cancel the contract or reject the goods. They must keep the goods, but they can sue the seller for monetary compensation (damages). For example, if you buy a new car and the paint on the door has a tiny scratch, this is a breach of warranty. You cannot return the car, but you can ask the seller to pay for a paint touch-up.

Implied Conditions (Sec 14)

There is a huge difference between Express Conditions (written specifically in the contract) and Implied Conditions (assumed by law). Even if the contract is silent on these points, the law automatically implies the following conditions in every sale:

  • Condition as to Title (Sec 14(a)): The seller must have the absolute legal right to sell the goods. If the seller is a thief, or if the goods belong to someone else, the buyer can cancel the contract and get their money back.
  • Condition as to Description (Sec 14(1)): If you buy goods based on a written description, the goods must strictly match that description. If you order a "Leather Jacket" and receive a "PU Leather Jacket", it is a breach of condition.
  • Condition as to Quality or Fitness (Sec 14(2)(b)): If a buyer specifically tells the seller the exact purpose for which they need the goods, and relies on the seller's skill and judgement to buy them, the goods must be reasonably fit for that purpose. For example, if you tell a medicine shop that you need a tube of cream for severe skin rashes, and they give you a cream that actually worsens the rash, they have breached the implied condition of fitness.
  • Condition as to Merchantable Quality (Sec 14(2)(c)): If you buy goods by description from a seller who deals in those types of goods, they must be of "merchantable quality". This simply means the goods should be in a saleable, usable condition. If you buy a bag of potatoes and half of them are rotten, they are not merchantable.
  • Condition in Sale by Sample (Sec 17): If you buy goods by looking at a sample, the bulk of the goods must correspond exactly to the sample in quality, and the buyer must have a reasonable opportunity to compare the bulk with the sample.
⚠️ The Famous Doctrine: Caveat Emptor (Sec 16)

Caveat Emptor is a Latin term that means "Let the buyer beware." In normal circumstances, it is the duty of the buyer to inspect the goods and ensure they are satisfactory. If a buyer buys a defective product without asking the seller for a guarantee, the seller is not liable.

Exceptions to Caveat Emptor (When the rule flips): Today, this doctrine is highly restricted. The seller becomes liable (Caveat Venditor) in these 6 cases:

  • When the buyer makes a known, specific purpose known to the seller, and relies on the seller's skill (Implied Condition of Fitness).
  • When the seller makes a fraudulent misrepresentation to hide a defect.
  • When the seller deliberately conceals a "latent defect" (a defect that the buyer cannot possibly notice upon ordinary examination).
  • When the goods are sold under a "patent" or trade name, relying on the manufacturer's skill.
  • When there is an implied condition of merchantable quality.
  • When the buyer checks the goods, and the seller refuses to let them examine fully.

4.3 Transfer of Ownership / Sale by Non-Owners

One of the most fundamental rules of property law is found in Sec 27. The rule is "Nemo dat quod non habet", which translates to "No one can give what they do not possess." This means that if a seller does not actually own the goods, they cannot transfer legal ownership to the buyer. If you buy a stolen television from a thief, you are technically not the legal owner—the original owner has the legal right to come and take it back from you, even if you paid for it.

However, the law provides strict exceptions to the Nemo dat rule where a buyer can actually get a valid title even if the seller is not the original owner. These are the exceptions you must memorize for your 6-mark answers:

  • Sale by Mercantile Agent (Sec 27): If a mercantile agent (like a broker or a commercial agent) sells goods in the ordinary course of their business with the consent of the owner, the buyer gets a valid title to the goods.
  • Sale by Co-owner (Sec 28): If two people jointly own goods, and one of them sells the entire goods with the consent of the other, the buyer gets a proper title.
  • Sale by a Person with a Voidable Title (Sec 29): If the seller obtained the goods through fraud, but the contract has not yet been rescinded (cancelled) by the original owner, and the seller sells the goods to an innocent buyer before the fraud is discovered, the buyer gets a valid title.
  • Sale by an Unpaid Seller (Sec 54): We will discuss this in detail in the next section. If an unpaid seller regains possession of goods and resells them, the new buyer gets a perfect title.
  • Sale under the Estoppel Principle: If the original owner, through their actions or negligence, allows someone else to appear as the owner, they are "estopped" (stopped) from later claiming their ownership over an innocent buyer.

4.4 Performance of the Contract of Sale

Once the contract of sale is made, both parties have to "perform" their duties. The seller is legally bound to deliver the goods, and the buyer is legally bound to accept the goods and pay the agreed price. The act lays down a series of strict rules for delivery of goods (Sec 31 to 44).

Rules regarding Delivery:

  • Delivery (Sec 2): Delivery means the voluntary transfer of possession from the seller to the buyer. It can be actual (physically handing over), constructive (handing over the keys to a warehouse), or symbolic (handing over documents like a bill of lading).
  • Place of Delivery (Sec 36): Unless agreed otherwise, the goods must be delivered at the seller's place of business.
  • Time of Delivery (Sec 36): If no time is fixed, the seller must deliver within a reasonable time.
  • Cost of Delivery (Sec 36): All expenses related to putting the goods into a deliverable state are borne by the seller.
  • Delivery of Wrong Quantity (Sec 37): If the seller delivers more, less, or mixed goods, the buyer has the right to accept the correct portion and reject the rest, or completely reject the whole delivery if they wish to.

4.5 Unpaid Seller (Meaning & Rights)

Suppose you run a wholesale shop. You give a retailer goods worth ₹2,00,000 on credit, but they refuse to pay you. Under the law, you become an "Unpaid Seller". An unpaid seller is defined under Section 45 as a seller to whom the full price of the goods has not been paid, or who has received payment in the form of a bounced cheque.

When the property (ownership) has not yet been transferred to the buyer, the law gives the unpaid seller three powerful rights against the goods themselves (as specified in your syllabus and PYQ):

  • 1. Right of Lien (Sec 47): Lien means the right to retain possession. If the seller is still in physical possession of the goods, they have the right to keep the goods until the buyer pays the full price. This is a very strong right, and the seller does not have to return the goods until the money is paid in full.
  • 2. Right of Stoppage in Transit (Sec 50): This is a fascinating legal right. If the seller has already dispatched the goods and handed them over to a carrier (like a trucking company or railways), but the goods are still "in transit" (they haven't reached the buyer yet), the seller has the right to stop the goods, instruct the carrier to return them, and retain possession until the buyer pays.
  • 3. Right of Resale (Sec 54): If the seller has properly exercised their right of lien or stoppage in transit, and the buyer still refuses to pay, the seller can finally sell the goods to a third party. If the seller resells the goods at a loss, they can sue the original defaulting buyer to recover the deficit. If they resell at a profit, they can keep the profit (this was not allowed earlier, but under modern interpretation, they can keep the profit if they gave proper notice to the buyer).
💡 Rights against goods VS. Rights against buyer

Important: The rights listed above (Lien, Stoppage, Resale) are against the goods. However, the unpaid seller also has rights against the buyer personally. These include suing the buyer for the price of the goods (Sec 55), or suing the buyer for damages for refusing to accept the goods (Sec 56). In your exams, if the question asks for "Rights against the goods", answer only Lien, Stoppage, and Resale.

📝 Exam Prep & PYQ Analysis (6+6+6 Marks)

This unit is a goldmine in DU exams because it produces highly predictable 6-mark questions. Here are the exact PYQs from your screenshots, broken down step-by-step with expert exam structure.

PYQ Analysis (6+6+6 Marks)

Q(a) Differentiate between sale and agreement to sell under the Sales of Goods Act, 1930.

Expert Exam Structure: Draw the exact comparison table provided in section 4.1 of these notes. Start with a brief introduction describing Sec 4(3) and Sec 4(4) (1 Mark). Then write the table (4 Marks). End with a practical example of how a buyer in a normal retail store gets immediate transfer of ownership, whereas a custom-order furniture buyer gets it at a future date (1 Mark).

Q(b) Define the terms condition and warranties under the Sales of Goods Act, 1930. Also discuss the implied conditions.

Expert Exam Structure:

  • Definitions (2 Marks): Define a Condition (Sec 12(2)) as an essential stipulation, breach of which gives the right to reject the goods. Define a Warranty (Sec 12(3)) as a collateral stipulation, breach of which only gives the right to claim damages, but NOT to reject.
  • Implied Conditions (4 Marks): List and explain:
    • Condition as to Title (Sec 14(a)).
    • Condition as to Description (Sec 14(1)).
    • Condition as to Quality/Fitness for a specific purpose (Sec 14(2)(b)).
    • Condition as to Merchantable Quality (Sec 14(2)(c)).
    • Sale by Sample (Sec 17).

Q(c) What is meant by 'Caveat Emptor'? Explain the exceptions to the doctrine of caveat emptor under the Sales of Goods Act, 1930.

Expert Exam Structure:

  • Meaning (1 Mark): "Caveat Emptor" is a Latin term meaning "Let the buyer beware". Under Sec 16, it is the buyer's absolute duty to examine the goods and ensure they are of good quality.
  • Exceptions (4 Marks): Explain the 6 exceptions where this rule flips to "Let the seller beware":
    • When the buyer relies on the seller's skill for a specific purpose.
    • When the seller fraudulently misrepresents the product.
    • When the seller conceals a latent defect (hidden defect).
    • When the seller sells under a patent/trade name.
    • When the goods are unmerchantable (not fit to be sold).
    • When the seller intentionally prevents the buyer from examining the goods.
  • Conclusion (1 Mark): Modern consumer laws heavily favour the buyer, making the seller increasingly liable for product defects.
PYQ Analysis (Nemo Dat & Unpaid Seller)

Q(a) "A seller cannot transfer to the buyer of goods a title better than what he himself has." Comment, giving exceptions to the rule.

Expert Exam Structure:

  • Rule (2 Marks): This is the famous "Nemo dat quod non habet" rule codified in Sec 27. It means "No one can give what they do not have". If a seller does not legally own the goods, they cannot legally transfer them.
  • Exceptions (4 Marks): The buyer still gets a valid title if:
    • The sale is made by a mercantile agent (Sec 27).
    • The sale is made by a co-owner with consent (Sec 28).
    • The sale is made by a person with a voidable title (Sec 29).
    • The sale is made by an unpaid seller exercising their right to re-sell (Sec 54).
    • The original owner is estopped (stopped) from denying the seller's right to sell.

Q(b) Discuss the rights of unpaid seller against the goods where property in goods has not transferred to the buyer.

Expert Exam Structure:

  • Meaning of Unpaid Seller (1 Mark): Under Sec 45, a seller is unpaid if the full price is not paid, or if a cheque given as payment has bounced.
  • Rights against the Goods (5 Marks): If the buyer defaults and the property (ownership) is still legally with the seller, the seller can exercise:
    • Right of Lien (Sec 47): Retain possession of the goods and refuse to deliver them until the price is paid.
    • Right of Stoppage in Transit (Sec 50): Stop the goods from reaching the buyer if they are in the hands of a common carrier.
    • Right of Resale (Sec 54): If the buyer still doesn't pay after notice, the seller can re-sell the goods to a third party and recover any shortfall from the defaulting buyer.
