Glossary & Exam Strategy

All key terms, answer-writing tips, time management, and thematic connections for high-scoring answers.

📖 Alphabetical Glossary of Key Terms

  • Alienation (Marx): The estrangement of workers from their labour, from the products they create, and from their own human nature under capitalism. Associated with Karl Marx.
  • Anarchism: A political tradition that rejects all forms of hierarchical authority, especially the state. Key thinkers: Proudhon, Bakunin, Kropotkin.
  • Behaviouralism: A movement in political science advocating for the empirical, value-free study of political behaviour. Key figure: David Easton.
  • Civil Disobedience: Deliberate, public, non-violent breaking of an unjust law to protest against it. Key figures: Thoreau, Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr.
  • Conservatism: A political tradition that values tradition, order, and gradual change. Key thinker: Edmund Burke.
  • Deconstruction (Derrida): A postmodern method of showing that texts have unstable meanings and that binary oppositions (like male/female, true/false) are always hierarchical.
  • Empirical Approach: The study of politics based on observable facts and data. It asks "what is" rather than "what ought to be".
  • Feminism: A critical perspective that analyses how gender shapes power and institutions, and advocates for equality between the sexes. Key thinkers: Wollstonecraft, Beauvoir.
  • General Will (Rousseau): The collective will of the people directed towards the common good. It is not the sum of individual wills, but the will of the community as a whole.
  • Grand Narratives (Lyotard): Overarching stories that claim to explain all of history and society (e.g., liberalism, Marxism). Postmodernism is sceptical of them.
  • Harm Principle (Mill): The principle that the only justification for restricting an individual's liberty is to prevent harm to others.
  • Historical Materialism (Marx): The view that the material conditions of production shape the social, political, and intellectual life of a society.
  • Legitimacy: The belief that a government has the right to rule and that its authority is justified.
  • Liberalism: A political tradition that emphasises individual freedom, rights, and limited government. Key thinkers: Locke, Mill, Rawls.
  • Marxism: A tradition that critiques capitalism as exploitative and advocates for revolutionary transformation towards a classless society. Key thinker: Karl Marx.
  • Negative Liberty (Berlin): Freedom from external interference or coercion.
  • Normative Approach: The study of political theory focused on values, ideals, and prescriptions — what ought to be.
  • Patriarchy: A system of social organisation in which men hold the primary power and dominate in roles of political leadership, moral authority, and control of property.
  • Political Community: A group of people bound by shared political institutions, laws, and a sense of belonging, typically identified with the nation-state.
  • Political Obligation: The moral duty of citizens to obey the laws and commands of the state.
  • Positive Liberty (Berlin): The capacity to act upon one's own will, to be the master of one's own life. Often requires the removal of internal and external obstacles.
  • Postmodernism: A critical perspective that rejects grand narratives, absolute truth, and the Enlightenment project of reason and progress. Key figures: Lyotard, Foucault.
  • Power/Knowledge (Foucault): The idea that knowledge is always produced within power relations, and serves to discipline and control populations.
  • Social Contract: A hypothetical agreement between individuals to form a society and a government, surrendering some freedom for security and order. Key thinkers: Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau.
  • Sovereignty: The supreme and undivided authority of a state within its territory.
  • Surplus Value (Marx): The difference between the value a worker produces and their wage — the source of capitalist profit and exploitation.
  • The Political: The fundamental character of political life, concerned with power, authority, conflict, and collective decision-making.

Tip: In your answers, always define key terms. Using the exact thinker associated with a concept (e.g., "Mill's harm principle") shows depth.

📝 Exam Strategy — Writing High-Scoring Answers

1. Time Allocation (3-hour paper)

  • 5-mark questions: 5-7 minutes each. Keep it crisp — 1 paragraph with definition + 1 example.
  • 10-mark questions: 12-15 minutes each. 3-4 paragraphs. Include a thinker and a critical point.
  • 15-mark questions: 20-22 minutes each. 5-6 paragraphs. Include multiple thinkers, a debate, and a conclusion.
  • 20-mark questions: 30-35 minutes each. 7-8 paragraphs. Full essay structure: intro, 4-5 substantive points with thinker references, critical evaluation, conclusion.
  • Reserve 5-10 minutes at the end for revision.

2. Structure Templates

  • For "Define/Explain" questions (5-10 marks):
    Definition → Elaboration (2-3 points) → Example → Brief critical point (if applicable).
  • For "Discuss/Critically Discuss" questions (15-20 marks):
    Introduction (define the topic, state your thesis) → Point 1 (with thinker) → Point 2 (with thinker) → Point 3 (maybe counter-argument) → Critical evaluation (limitations or debates) → Conclusion (summarise and give your balanced view).
  • For "Compare and Contrast" questions:
    Introduction → Similarities (with examples) → Differences (with examples, using a table if possible) → Which view is stronger? → Conclusion.

3. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not defining key terms: Always start by defining the central concept (e.g., "Political obligation is the moral duty to obey the state...").
  • Writing long, rambling paragraphs: Break your answer into clear, logical paragraphs. Each paragraph should have a topic sentence.
  • Forgetting thinkers' names: Always attribute arguments to specific thinkers (e.g., "As Locke argued..." or "Feminists like Mary Wollstonecraft...").
  • Ignoring critical evaluation: A "discuss" question requires you to show both sides and give a balanced view. Don't just summarise.
  • Not using examples: Examples show that you understand the theory in practice. Use current events, historical events, or everyday situations.
  • Running out of time: Practice writing timed answers. Stick to the time allocation above.

4. High-Impact Scholarly Terms to Weave In

  • Zoon politikon (Aristotle's political animal)
  • Virtù (Machiavelli's political skill)
  • Verantwortungsethik (Weber's ethic of responsibility)
  • Surplus value (Marx)
  • General Will (Rousseau)
  • Negative/Positive liberty (Berlin)
  • Power/knowledge (Foucault)
  • Harm principle (Mill)
  • Dictatorship of the proletariat (Marx)
  • Incredulity towards metanarratives (Lyotard)

Tip: Using these terms naturally in your answers signals to the examiner that you are well-read and conceptually precise.
