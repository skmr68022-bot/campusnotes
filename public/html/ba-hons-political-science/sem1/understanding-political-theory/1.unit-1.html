Theorizing the ‘Political’

A. What is this Unit About? — The Big Picture

When we hear the word "politics", most of us immediately think of politicians, elections, Parliament, or maybe the news debates on TV. But political theory goes much deeper. It asks a far more fundamental question: What actually makes something 'political' in the first place? Is a family deciding who does the dishes a political decision? Is a group of friends choosing where to go for dinner political? What about a company CEO firing an employee — is that political?

This unit is all about exploring these boundaries. It introduces you to the idea that politics is not just about governments and laws; it is about power, conflict, cooperation, and collective decision-making in every group where humans live together. Some thinkers (like Max Weber) believe politics is confined to the state and its monopoly on violence. Others (like feminists and postmodernists) argue that politics is everywhere — in the home, in the workplace, even in our personal relationships. This unit will give you the tools to think critically about these different views, and to understand that how we define 'the political' has huge consequences for who gets power, who is excluded, and how we imagine a just society.

In exams, questions from this unit are frequent and often carry high marks. You might be asked to define politics, to discuss the nature of the political, or to compare how different thinkers have theorised the political. Mastery of this unit is essential because it forms the foundation for everything else you will study in political theory.

B. Key Concepts — Explained in Depth

Before we dive into the thinkers, let's get our vocabulary straight. These are the core terms you must know and use in your answers.

  • Politics (राजनीति): Politics is the activity through which people make, preserve, and amend the general rules under which they live. It involves conflict, cooperation, and the exercise of power to allocate scarce resources and values. In everyday life, politics happens whenever a group of people has to make a collective decision — from a family planning a vacation to a nation passing a budget. As Andrew Heywood puts it, politics is "the activity through which people make, preserve and amend the general rules under which they live". It is not just about who holds office; it is about the dynamics of rule-making and rule-enforcement in any human group.
  • The Political (राजनीतिक): This is a more abstract and philosophical category than 'politics'. While 'politics' refers to the activities and processes, 'the political' asks: What is the essence of political life? What makes a situation or a relationship 'political' as opposed to 'social', 'economic', or 'private'? For some, the political is defined by conflict (Schmitt). For others, it is defined by the pursuit of the common good (Aristotle). Theorizing the political is about drawing the boundaries of the political realm — deciding what belongs inside and what stays outside.
  • Political Theory (राजनीतिक सिद्धांत): This is the systematic study of the ideas, concepts, and principles that inform political life. It is both descriptive (explaining how political institutions and practices work) and normative (prescribing how they ought to work). Political theory is not just a collection of opinions; it is a rigorous, reflective inquiry into questions of justice, rights, liberty, and power. Isaiah Berlin described political theory as "obstinately philosophical", meaning it always involves deep, contestable questions about values.
  • State (राज्य): The state is a political association that establishes sovereign jurisdiction within defined territorial boundaries. It exercises legitimate authority over a population through a set of institutions — government, bureaucracy, police, military, and courts. The state is the primary locus of the political in modern thought because it claims the ultimate authority to make and enforce laws. Without the state, there is no overarching authority to settle disputes — which is why thinkers like Hobbes saw the state as essential for peace.
  • Power (सत्ता): Power is the capacity to influence, control, or shape the behaviour of others, even against their will. It is one of the most central categories in theorizing the political. Power can be coercive (using force or threats), structural (embedded in institutions and systems), ideological (shaping people's beliefs and values), or productive (creating new identities and ways of being). Thinkers like Max Weber saw power as the ability to get things done; feminists like Hannah Arendt saw power as the capacity to act in concert, not domination.
  • Sovereignty (प्रभुसत्ता): Sovereignty is the principle of supreme and undivided authority within a territory. It is the defining feature of the modern state. A sovereign state is one that recognises no higher authority above it — it is the ultimate source of law and order within its borders. This concept is crucial for understanding why states are considered the primary actors in international relations, and why they claim a monopoly on legitimate violence.
  • Normative vs. Empirical (आदर्शात्मक बनाम अनुभवजन्य): Normative refers to values, ideals, and prescriptions about how things ought to be. Empirical refers to observable facts and causal explanations about how things actually are. In political theory, normative questions ask: "What is justice?"; empirical questions ask: "How do people actually vote?" This distinction runs throughout the course.

Everyday example of these concepts: Imagine a group of five friends trying to decide which movie to watch. Politics is the process of discussion, bargaining, and voting. The political is the underlying power dynamic — maybe one friend always gets their way because they are louder, or because they own the TV. The state is like the ultimate referee (or the parent) who has the final say. Power is the influence that the loudest friend has. Sovereignty is the parent's ultimate authority to override the decision. Normative questions would be: "What is a fair way to choose?"; Empirical questions would be: "Who usually ends up choosing in this group?"

C. Thinkers and Their Contributions — Full Profiles

1. Aristotle (384–322 BCE) — The Political Animal

Major Works: Politics, Nicomachean Ethics

Aristotle is often considered the father of political science because he was the first to treat politics as a distinct subject of systematic study. His starting point is both simple and profound: man is by nature a political animal (zoon politikon). By this, Aristotle meant that humans are not meant to live alone like beasts or gods; we are naturally drawn to live in communities, and we can only achieve our full potential — our happiness and virtue — within a political community (the polis). For Aristotle, the polis is not just a convenience; it is a natural necessity. The individual is not self-sufficient; we need others to survive, to thrive, and to become fully human.

Aristotle famously argued that the polis is prior to the individual, just as the whole is prior to the part. A hand cut off from the body is no longer a hand; similarly, a human cut off from political community is no longer fully human. This is a deeply communitarian vision of politics — the community is not just a collection of individuals; it is the context in which individuals become who they are.

Aristotle also saw politics as the "master science" — the discipline that determines which other sciences should be studied in the state, and that guides the overall pursuit of the good life. For him, the purpose of politics is not just to maintain order, but to enable citizens to live virtuously and happily. The best constitution is the one that promotes the common good and cultivates civic virtue.

Everyday Example: Think of a cricket team. A player cannot win a match alone; they need the team. The team (like the polis) is prior to the individual player. The rules of the game (like the constitution) structure how the team plays. The goal is not just to score runs, but to win together — that is the common good.

Key Quotation: "Man is by nature a political animal." — Politics, Book I. This is not just a description; it is a normative claim. Humans fulfil their essential nature only through participation in political community.

Critical Limitation: Aristotle's vision is deeply exclusionary. He believed that women, slaves, and 'barbarians' (non-Greeks) were not capable of full political participation because they lacked the requisite rationality or virtue. For Aristotle, democracy was also dangerous because it could degenerate into mob rule. His ideal was a mixed constitution (polity) based on a strong middle class. Today, we see these views as deeply biased and elitist.

2. Niccolò Machiavelli (1469–1527) — The Realist of Power

Major Works: The Prince, Discourses on Livy

Machiavelli is often seen as the first modern political thinker because he broke decisively with the moral and religious frameworks of medieval political thought. He did not describe an ideal, Utopian state; he described how politics actually works, based on his experience as a diplomat in Renaissance Florence. Machiavelli's central insight is that politics is an autonomous sphere with its own rules — it cannot be reduced to morality or religion. A ruler must sometimes do evil to maintain the stability and security of the state.

Machiavelli introduced the concept of virtù — the ability of a ruler to act decisively, cunningly, and ruthlessly when necessary. This is not moral virtue; it is political skill. A successful prince must be like a lion (to frighten the wolves) and a fox (to avoid the snares). He must be willing to break his promises, to lie, and to use cruelty if it serves the state's survival. However, Machiavelli was not amoral; he argued that the ends of statecraft — security, stability, and glory — justify the means. The prince's ultimate duty is to preserve the state, because the state is the precondition for any kind of civilized life.

In the Discourses, Machiavelli also celebrated republicanism — the active participation of citizens in self-government. He admired the Roman Republic and believed that a mixed constitution (with elements of monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy) was the most stable form of government.

Everyday Example: Imagine a start-up CEO who has to lay off 100 employees to save the company from bankruptcy. Morally, it seems wrong; politically, it might be necessary. Or think of a government that imposes a lockdown during a pandemic — it restricts freedoms, but it is done to save lives. Machiavelli would say: "If the end is survival, the means are justified."

Key Quotation: "The ends justify the means." — The Prince, Ch. 18 (paraphrased). Machiavelli was not advocating amorality for its own sake; he was arguing that political action operates in a realm where ethical considerations must sometimes yield to the necessity of preserving the state.

Critical Limitation: Machiavelli's reputation has suffered from being seen as a teacher of tyranny. Critics argue that his advice to rulers is cynical and dangerous, and that it undermines the moral foundations of political life. However, others read him as a republican thinker who wanted to expose the hypocrisy of rulers and to warn citizens against the dangers of princely rule.

3. Max Weber (1864–1920) — The State as Monopoly of Violence

Major Works: Politics as a Vocation, Economy and Society

Max Weber was a German sociologist who gave one of the most famous definitions of the modern state. For Weber, the state is that human community which claims the monopoly on the legitimate use of physical force within a given territory. This is a very specific, modern definition. In the past, there were many actors who could use violence — feudal lords, private armies, bandits. But the modern state claims to be the only entity that can legitimately use violence. This is what makes it a state.

Weber also defined politics as the struggle for power, or the influencing of the distribution of power. Political action is ultimately about power — who gets it, how it is exercised, and how it is justified. For Weber, legitimacy is crucial. He identified three ideal types of legitimate authority: traditional (based on custom and habit), charismatic (based on the exceptional qualities of a leader), and legal-rational (based on the belief in the legality of enacted rules and the right of those in authority to issue commands). The modern state rests on legal-rational authority — we obey the law, not the person, and the law is made through rational procedures.

Weber also made a famous distinction between the ethic of responsibility (Verantwortungsethik) and the ethic of ultimate ends (Gesinnungsethik). The politician must act with an ethic of responsibility — they must consider the consequences of their actions, and they cannot simply cling to moral purity. A politician who says "I will never lie, even if it destroys my country" is following an ethic of ultimate ends, and Weber considered that irresponsible. The responsible politician calculates the consequences and may have to compromise their principles for the sake of the public good.

Everyday Example: Only the police (state) can legally arrest you; your neighbour cannot. If your neighbour tries to put you in jail, that is illegitimate violence. Also, think of a politician who supports a tax increase even though it will be unpopular because they believe it is necessary for the country's economic stability — that is an ethic of responsibility.

Key Quotation: "Politics is a strong and slow boring of hard boards." — Politics as a Vocation. This means politics is demanding, pragmatic, and often frustrating. It requires passion, judgement, and a sense of proportion.

Critical Limitation: Weber's definition of the state has been criticised for being too state-centric. It ignores the political dimensions of civil society, global governance, and the private sphere. Feminist critics, for example, argue that the "private" realm of the household is also a site of political power, but Weber's definition leaves it out.

4. Hannah Arendt (1906–1975) — The Politics of Action and Speech

Major Works: The Human Condition, On Revolution

Hannah Arendt offers a radical, beautiful, and deeply inspiring vision of politics. She rejected the idea that politics is about ruling, dominating, or managing. Instead, she argued that politics is the realm of action, speech, and plurality. It is the space where human beings come together as equals, appear to one another, and create a common world through words and deeds. For Arendt, the political is the sphere of freedom — not freedom from something, but freedom to act, to initiate, to begin something new.

Arendt was deeply concerned about what she called the "rise of the social" — the tendency of modern society to reduce everything to economic and administrative concerns. In ancient Greece, the polis was separate from the household (oikos). The household was the realm of necessity, of labour, of survival. The polis was the realm of freedom, where citizens discussed and decided on the common affairs of the city. But in modernity, Arendt argued, the social has eclipsed the political. We now treat politics like a big household — we worry about administration, management, and economic growth, but we have lost the space for genuine political action and public deliberation.

Arendt also had a distinctive view of power. For her, power is not domination; it is the capacity to act in concert. Power arises when people come together and agree to act collectively. It is based on persuasion, not violence. This is a very different view from Weber's or Machiavelli's. For Arendt, violence is the opposite of power — when violence is used, power has already failed.

Everyday Example: A student council meeting where students debate the budget for a cultural festival, listen to each other, and then vote. That is Arendt-style politics — it's about persuasion, plural voices, and collective decision-making. When the principal imposes a decision without discussion, that is not politics; it is administration or domination.

Key Quotation: "To be political, to live in a polis, meant that everything was decided through words and persuasion and not through force and violence." — The Human Condition. For Arendt, this is the essence of authentic politics.

Critical Limitation: Arendt's sharp distinction between the political and the social has been criticised for being too idealised and elitist. She seems to assume that only a privileged few (male citizens in ancient Greece) could engage in politics. What about the struggles of women, workers, and minorities for recognition and rights? Are these not political? Feminist and Marxist critics argue that Arendt's framework excludes the very struggles that are most urgent.

5. Carl Schmitt (1888–1985) — The Politics of Friend and Enemy

Major Works: The Concept of the Political

Carl Schmitt is one of the most controversial and provocative political thinkers of the 20th century. He offered a stark, conflict-oriented definition of the political. For Schmitt, the specific political distinction is that between friend and enemy. This is not a metaphor; it is an existential distinction. The enemy is not necessarily a personal adversary; the enemy is the "other" who represents a different way of life, a different identity, and whose existence poses a threat to our own. Politics, for Schmitt, is the domain of the most intense antagonism — the possibility of killing and being killed for one's community.

Schmitt argued that the political is not reducible to economics, morality, or aesthetics. Each of these domains has its own distinctions: good/evil (morality), beautiful/ugly (aesthetics), profitable/unprofitable (economics). But the political has its own autonomous criterion: friend/enemy. When a conflict becomes so intense that it threatens the very existence of a community, it becomes political.

Schmitt also introduced the concept of the sovereign as the one who decides on the exception. In normal times, laws and procedures apply. But in a state of emergency (a war, a revolution, a natural disaster), the sovereign must step outside the law and make a decision to protect the community. This decision is what defines sovereignty. For Schmitt, the liberal state, with its endless legal procedures and checks and balances, is weak because it cannot deal effectively with existential threats.

Everyday Example: India-Pakistan relations are a clear example of the friend/enemy distinction. When there is a terrorist attack or a military standoff, the political becomes intense. The identity of "us" (Indians) is sharply contrasted with "them" (Pakistanis). This is Schmitt's political in action.

Key Quotation: "The specific political distinction to which political actions and motives can be reduced is that between friend and enemy." — The Concept of the Political. For Schmitt, this defines the political in its most concrete and existential sense.

Critical Limitation: Schmitt's thought has been widely criticised for legitimising authoritarianism, exceptionalism, and the suppression of pluralism. His friend-enemy distinction can be used to justify any form of violent exclusion. He was a Nazi sympathiser, and his legal theories were used to justify Hitler's dictatorship. However, his concepts have been influential in contemporary political theory, particularly in discussions of sovereignty, emergency powers, and the limits of liberalism.

D. Core Debates — In-Depth Discussions

Debate 1: Is Politics Confined to the State, or Does It Extend to All Social Relations?

Proposition (State-centric view): Politics is fundamentally about the state — the institutions, laws, and processes through which binding decisions are made for a society. This view is most powerfully articulated by Max Weber, who defines politics in relation to the state's monopoly on legitimate violence. David Easton also offers a state-centric definition: politics is the "authoritative allocation of values" — the key word being "authoritative", which implies the state's authority. In this view, the family, the workplace, and civil society are not political; they are private or social spheres. Politics happens in government, in parliament, in courts.

Counter-proposition (Broad view): Politics pervades all social relations where power, conflict, and collective decision-making occur. Feminist theorists famously argued that "the personal is political" — domestic violence, reproductive rights, and the division of household labour are not private issues; they are tied to larger structures of patriarchy and power. Michel Foucault showed that power operates through discourse, discipline, and biopower across all spheres of life — in schools, hospitals, prisons, and even in the way we think about sexuality. Antonio Gramsci emphasised the political nature of civil society through his concept of hegemony — the idea that the ruling class maintains control not just through force, but through ideas, culture, and institutions. In this view, politics is everywhere there is power.

Everyday Example: A company policy that says women must wear uniforms while men can wear casual clothes is a political issue — it involves power, gender norms, and discrimination. It is not just a "private" company matter. Similarly, a family decision about who takes care of elderly parents is a political issue because it reflects broader societal norms about gender and care work.

Synthesis: Most contemporary theorists recognise that while the state is a crucial locus of politics, politics operates in multiple sites — from the household to the global arena, from social movements to digital platforms. The state-centric view is too narrow, but the "everything is political" view can be too broad. The best approach is to see the political as a dimension of social life that can emerge whenever power and collective decision-making are at stake.

Debate 2: Is Politics About Conflict or Cooperation?

Proposition (Conflict view): Politics is fundamentally about the resolution of conflict over scarce resources, values, and power. Carl Schmitt argues that the political is defined by the friend-enemy distinction — the most intense antagonism. Marxist traditions see politics as the arena of class struggle and the exercise of state power in the interest of the ruling class. Even Machiavelli portrayed politics as a struggle for power, where the ends justify the means. In this view, politics is inescapably conflictual; it is about who gets what, when, and how.

Counter-proposition (Cooperation view): Politics is the activity of reconciling differences through deliberation, bargaining, and shared understanding. Bernard Crick defines politics as "a distinctive form of rule where people come together through institutional mechanism to deliberate and resolve differences". Hannah Arendt sees politics as the space of action and speech where citizens create a common world through persuasion, not force. In this view, politics is the art of peaceful conflict resolution; it is what makes violence unnecessary.

Everyday Example: A family deciding how to split the household chores is a conflict — everyone wants to do the least work. If they sit down, discuss, and agree on a schedule, that is cooperative politics. If the father simply orders everyone around, that is domination, not politics. The ideal of politics is that we manage our conflicts through dialogue, not through violence.

Synthesis: Most contemporary theorists recognise that politics involves both conflict and cooperation. There is no politics without conflict (otherwise there would be nothing to decide), and there is no politics without cooperation (otherwise there would be only war). Politics is the ongoing process of managing and transforming conflicts through institutional mechanisms that may be more or less cooperative.

Debate 3: The Political vs. The Social — Are They Distinct?

Proposition (Distinct spheres): The political and the social are qualitatively different domains. Hannah Arendt is the most famous exponent of this view. She argued that the rise of the social (economic and administrative concerns) has displaced the properly political, leading to the decline of authentic politics. The political is the realm of freedom, action, and plurality; the social is the realm of necessity, labour, and administration. Ancient Greeks kept the household (oikos) separate from the polis. Modernity has collapsed this distinction, which Arendt saw as a disaster.

Counter-proposition (Overlapping spheres): The distinction between the political and the social is untenable. Feminist and Marxist critics argue that what counts as 'social' (e.g., family, economy, culture) is always already shaped by power relations and is therefore political. The boundaries between public and private are themselves a product of political struggles. Carl Schmitt also argued that the political can emerge from any domain — economics, religion, culture — when the friend-enemy distinction is activated. There is no fixed, a-political sphere.

Synthesis: While analytical distinctions can be useful, contemporary political theory largely rejects a rigid separation. The political is not a fixed sphere but a dimension that can emerge in any social relation where collective decisions, power, and conflict are at stake. The best approach is to see the political as a horizon — it is always potentially present in any social interaction.

F. Unit Summary — Must-Know Points for Revision

  • Politics is the collective activity of making and enforcing binding decisions; the political is the deeper philosophical question of what constitutes the political realm.
  • Aristotle grounds politics in human nature — we are political animals who flourish only in community. He sees politics as a positive, virtue-enhancing activity.
  • Machiavelli separates politics from morality — politics has its own autonomous logic of power and necessity. He is a realist who focuses on how politics actually works.
  • Weber defines the modern state by its monopoly on legitimate violence and distinguishes between ethics of responsibility and ultimate ends.
  • Arendt recovers politics as a space of freedom, action, and persuasion — distinct from the social realm of necessity. She sees politics as a noble, creative activity.
  • Schmitt defines the political by the friend-enemy distinction — politics is the domain of existential conflict. His view is dark but influential.
  • Key debate: Is politics state-centred or pervasive? Is it about conflict or cooperation? The answers shape how we understand power, authority, and citizenship.
  • Key debate: Is the political distinct from the social, or are they inseparable? Most contemporary theorists lean towards the latter, recognising that power is everywhere.

G. Unit-Specific PYQs — With Detailed, Step-by-Step Model Outlines

Q1. (2019, 20 marks) "What is politics? Discuss with reference to theorizing the political."

Detailed Model Outline:

  • Introduction (4-5 lines): Begin by acknowledging that "politics" is a contested concept. In everyday language, it means elections, governments, and politicians. But in political theory, we go deeper. We ask: what is the essence of the political? Is it about the state, about power, about conflict, or about collective deliberation? This essay will explore these different dimensions.
  • Argument 1 — Politics as State-Centric (Weber): Max Weber defines politics in relation to the state's monopoly on legitimate violence. Politics is the struggle for power or the influencing of the distribution of power. The state is the ultimate political institution. This view is useful because it clearly identifies the institutional locus of politics. Example: When we talk about "national politics", we mean the state-level decisions on defence, taxation, and foreign policy.
  • Argument 2 — Politics as a Broad, Pervasive Activity (Feminism, Foucault): Feminist critics argue that the personal is political. Domestic violence, reproductive rights, and the division of household labour are deeply political issues. Michel Foucault shows that power operates everywhere — through discourse, discipline, and knowledge. This broad view expands our understanding of the political. Example: The #MeToo movement made sexual harassment a public political issue, not just a private matter.
  • Argument 3 — Politics as Conflict vs. Cooperation (Schmitt vs. Arendt): Schmitt defines the political by the friend-enemy distinction. Politics is the domain of existential conflict, where communities define themselves against others. In contrast, Arendt sees politics as the space of action, speech, and persuasion. For her, politics is about creating a common world, not about dominating enemies. Which view is more convincing? Most contemporary theorists would say both are present in different contexts.
  • Argument 4 — The Political vs. The Social (Arendt and critics): Arendt famously distinguished the political from the social. For her, the rise of the social (economic concerns, administration, management) has eroded authentic politics. But critics (feminists, Marxists) argue that the social is already political. There is no clear dividing line. This debate shows that the boundaries of the political are themselves a political question.
  • Conclusion (3-4 lines): Politics is not a single thing; it is a multifaceted concept. We cannot reduce it to one definition. Theorizing the political requires engaging with competing traditions: state-centric, pervasive, conflict-based, and cooperative. The richness of political theory lies in this pluralism. No single thinker has the final word.

High-Scoring Keywords: Zoon politikon Monopoly of violence Friend-enemy Rise of the social Hegemony Authoritative allocation Plurality Reconciliation

Q2. (2022, 10 marks) "Why is the State theorised as a political system?"

Detailed Model Outline:

  • Introduction: The state is the central institution of modern political life. It is theorised as a political system because it is the primary site of authoritative decision-making, sovereignty, and legitimate coercion. Without the state, there would be no overarching authority to make and enforce binding decisions for society.
  • Argument 1 — Weberian Definition: Max Weber defines the state as the entity that claims a monopoly on the legitimate use of physical force within a given territory. This makes it uniquely political because it is the only institution that can legitimately use violence to enforce its decisions. The state is the ultimate arbiter of disputes.
  • Argument 2 — Systemic Perspective (Easton): David Easton theorises the political system as a set of interactions through which authoritative values are allocated for society. The state is the core institutional apparatus of this system. It receives inputs (demands and support from citizens) and converts them into outputs (authoritative decisions like laws and policies). This systems-theory approach shows how the state functions as a dynamic, interactive system.
  • Argument 3 — Sovereignty: The state exercises supreme authority over a population. It is the ultimate locus of political decision-making, distinguishing it from other social institutions (family, church, corporations). This sovereignty is what makes the state a political system — it is the source of the highest authority.
  • Conclusion: The state is theorised as a political system because it is the institutional embodiment of political authority, sovereignty, and the authoritative allocation of values in modern societies. It is the framework within which all other political processes operate.

High-Scoring Keywords: Legitimate violence Sovereignty Authoritative allocation Political system Institutional apparatus

Q3. (2018, 10 marks) "Do you think that power equation and conflict lie at the centre of the concept of 'political'?"

Detailed Model Outline:

  • Introduction: Power and conflict are undeniably central to many definitions of the political. However, they are not exhaustive. Some traditions emphasise cooperation, deliberation, and the common good. This answer will explore both sides.
  • Argument for "Yes" — Power and Conflict are Central (Schmitt, Marx, Machiavelli): Carl Schmitt defines the political by the friend-enemy distinction — the possibility of conflict is what makes something political. Machiavelli portrays politics as a struggle for power where the ends justify the means. Marx sees politics as class struggle, and the state as an instrument of class rule. These thinkers all centre power and conflict in their definitions of the political.
  • Argument for "No" — Politics is also Cooperation and Deliberation (Arendt, Crick): Hannah Arendt argues that authentic politics is about action, speech, and persuasion — not domination. Bernard Crick defines politics as a distinctive form of rule where people come together through institutional mechanisms to deliberate and resolve differences. For these thinkers, power is not the same as domination; it is the capacity to act in concert. Conflict is present, but the essence of politics is how we manage and resolve it peacefully.
  • Synthesis: Power and conflict are essential components of the political, but they do not capture the whole picture. Politics is also about cooperation, negotiation, and the creation of common meaning. A comprehensive understanding includes both dimensions.
  • Conclusion: Power and conflict are at the centre of the political, but they are not the only elements. The 'political' is a complex, contested concept that includes both the struggle for power and the striving for cooperative solutions.

High-Scoring Keywords: Friend-enemy Class struggle Action and speech Hegemony Deliberation Productive power
