1. Concept & Nature of Business Income

💰 Revenue (The "Top Line")

Revenue is the total gross inflow of economic benefits (cash, receivables, or other assets) during a specific period. It arises strictly in the ordinary course of business activities. It is the raw earning power of a business before any costs are deducted.

  • Operating Revenue: Sales of goods, fees for services.
  • Non-Operating Revenue: Interest received, dividend income, rent received.

📈 Business Income (The "Bottom Line")

Business Income (or Net Profit) is the net increase in a business's wealth. It is calculated by deducting all related expenses, costs of goods sold, depreciation, and operating losses from the total revenue. It represents the actual economic value added to the entity.

  • Operating Income: Profit generated directly from core business operations (EBIT).
  • Net Income: The final profit after all taxes, interest, and extraordinary items are paid.
🧠 Exam Cheat Code: R.G.I.N. — Revenue is Gross, Income is Net.

High Revenue Trap: High revenue does not guarantee high income. If operating expenses tripled while revenue only doubled, the business income could actually be negative (a net loss). Revenue shows scale; Income shows sustainability.

Fundamental Principles Governing Income

1. The Accrual Concept

Income is recognized when it is earned, and expenses when they are incurred, regardless of cash flow timing.

2. The Realization Principle

Revenue is only recognized when the earning process is virtually complete and an exchange has taken place.

3. The Matching Principle

To determine true income, you must "match" the expenses incurred during that period against the revenues generated in that same period.

2. Measurement of Business Income

The Periodicity Concept: We artificially slice the continuous business timeline into discrete 12-month intervals (Fiscal Years). This is essential because:

  • Performance Benchmarking: Allows stakeholders to compare performance across specific timelines.
  • Statutory Compliance: Tax authorities mandate annual filings to assess fiscal liabilities.
  • Strategic Agility: Managers require interim feedback to adjust pricing, cost structures, and operational strategies.
💡 Critical Exam Insight: Profit is NOT Cash Inflow minus Cash Outflow. Profit is Recognized Revenue minus the Expired Costs of generating that specific revenue.

3. Revenue Recognition (AS 9)

Accounting Standard 9 (AS 9) provides the authoritative guidelines for the timing of revenue recognition. Its primary goal is to ensure that income is recognized only when there is reasonable certainty that the ultimate collection of cash is assured and the significant risks/rewards of ownership have been transferred.

⚡ The Core Principle: Under AS 9, revenue is recognized when "Significant risks and rewards of ownership are transferred to the buyer," and there is no remaining effective control over the goods sold.
Revenue SourcePoint of Recognition (Trigger Point)Key Condition
Sale of GoodsAt the point of sale / transfer of property.Significant risks and rewards must pass to the buyer.
Rendering ServicesPercentage of Completion Method.Revenue is recognized proportionately based on the work completed.
Interest IncomeTime-proportion basis.Recognized based on the passage of time (accrual).
Royalties/DividendsAccrual / Receipt basis.Recognized when the right to receive is established.

When Should You Postpone Recognition?

  • Collection Uncertainty: The buyer's financial position is so poor that final payment is highly doubtful.
  • Performance Obligations: The seller still has to perform significant extra acts to complete the sale.
  • Return Risks: There is a high probability of goods being returned or rejected that cannot be reliably estimated.
4. Accounting for PPE (AS 10)

According to AS 10, Property, Plant, and Equipment (PPE) are tangible items held for use in the production or supply of goods/services, for rental to others, or for administrative purposes, and are expected to be used during more than one period.

ComponentWhat is included in the "Cost"?
Direct CostPurchase price, including non-refundable import duties and purchase taxes.
InstallationCosts directly attributable to bringing the asset to the location and condition necessary for it to operate as intended.
TestingCosts of testing whether the asset is functioning properly (after deducting net proceeds from sale of trial-run items).

Measurement Models

  • Cost Model: The asset is carried at its cost less any accumulated depreciation and accumulated impairment losses. This is the standard, conservative approach.
  • Revaluation Model: After recognition, the asset is carried at a revalued amount (its fair value at the date of revaluation).

Impact of Depreciation on Business Income

The Matching Principle

Depreciation ensures that the "consumption" of an asset is matched against the revenue it helps generate. Without depreciation, profit would be overstated in early years and understated in later years.

Impact on Taxable Income

Depreciation is a non-cash expense that reduces "Book Profit." Higher depreciation in the early years of an asset's life significantly lowers the corporate tax burden for that period.

Income Smoothing

By spreading costs, depreciation prevents large, erratic "lumpy" expenses from ruining the financial report card in the year an asset is purchased.

5. Accounting for Intangible Assets (AS 26)

Under AS 26, an intangible asset is a non-monetary, identifiable asset without physical substance. It is held for production, supply of goods/services, or administrative purposes.

Type of IntangibleTreatmentExplanation
Acquired IntangibleCapitalized at cost.Recognized at purchase price plus directly attributable costs.
Internally GeneratedExpensed (Usually).AS 26 prohibits capitalizing internal costs (like research/branding) because they are too subjective.
Amortization PeriodSystematic Allocation.Must be amortized over its best estimate of useful life (usually capped at 10 years if life is uncertain).
⚡ The R&D Boundary:
• Research Phase: Costs spent on investigation to gain new knowledge. Always expensed in the Profit & Loss account.
• Development Phase: Costs spent applying research to a plan or design. You capitalize these as an intangible asset only if you can prove technical feasibility, intention to complete, and ability to sell it.
6. Valuation of Inventory (AS 2)

Accounting Standard 2 (AS 2) dictates that inventory must be valued at the lower of Cost or Net Realizable Value (NRV). This rule is a direct application of the Prudence (Conservatism) Principle.

Overstated Closing Stock

If you value your closing inventory too high, COGS becomes artificially low. This results in an overstated profit (inflated business income), potentially leading to higher tax liabilities.

Understated Closing Stock

If you value inventory too low, COGS appears higher than it truly is. This leads to understated business income, which can hide the company's true performance from stakeholders.

MethodThe LogicBest Used When...
FIFOFirst-In, First-Out: The oldest items are sold first.Prices are rising; matches physical movement of perishable goods.
Weighted AverageCosts are smoothed by taking a total average.When individual units are interchangeable and hard to track individually.
LIFOLast-In, First-Out: The newest items are sold first.(Note: LIFO is prohibited by AS 2 and IFRS because it tends to undervalue inventory.)

PYQ-Style Question

On 1st April 2020, Mr. Sharma bought a machine for ₹5,00,000. He paid ₹50,000 for transportation and ₹30,000 for installation.

On 1st October 2021, he bought another machine for ₹3,00,000.

On 1st July 2022, he sold the first machine for ₹3,50,000.

On the same date (1st July 2022), he purchased a new machine for ₹2,50,000.

Depreciation is charged at 10% per annum on the Straight Line Method. Accounts are closed on 31st March every year.

Required: Prepare the Machinery Account for 2020‑21, 2021‑22, and 2022‑23.

Step 1 · Total cost of each machine
M1 (1‑Apr‑2020) = 5,00,000 + 50,000 + 30,000 = ₹5,80,000
M2 (1‑Oct‑2021) = ₹3,00,000 (no extra given)
M3 (1‑Jul‑2022) = ₹2,50,000
Step 2 · Depreciation year by year
2020‑21: M1 full year → 5,80,000 × 10% = ₹58,000
2021‑22: M1 full year ₹58,000 + M2 (6 months) = 3,00,000 × 10% × 6/12 = ₹15,000 → total ₹73,000
2022‑23: M1 sold on 1‑Jul → dep for 3 months = 5,80,000 × 10% × 3/12 = ₹14,500.
   WDV of M1 on sale = 5,80,000 – (58,000+58,000+14,500) = ₹4,49,500.
   Loss on sale = 4,49,500 – 3,50,000 = ₹99,500 (debit to P&L).
   M2 full year = 3,00,000 × 10% = ₹30,000.
   M3 (purchased 1‑Jul) → 9 months = 2,50,000 × 10% × 9/12 = ₹18,750.
   Total depreciation 2022‑23 = 14,500 + 30,000 + 18,750 = ₹63,250

📌 Quick Revision — Depreciation Exam Cheat Sheet:
• Machine purchased on 1st April → Full year depreciation
• Machine purchased on 1st October → Half year (6 months)
• Machine purchased on 1st July → 9 months (if year ends 31st March)
• Machine sold → Charge depreciation up to date of sale only
• Profit on sale → Credit to P&L; Loss on sale → Debit to P&L

📌 Quick Revision — Inventory Exam Cheat Sheet:
• FIFO: Sales take from oldest stock first; Closing stock from latest purchases.
• Weighted Average: Recalculate average after EVERY purchase.
• Normal Loss: Reduce quantity only (no cost impact).
• Abnormal Loss: Remove quantity AND cost; charge to P&L.
• Purchase Return: FIFO → reduce from latest purchase layer; Weighted Avg → reduce cost & units, recalculate average.
