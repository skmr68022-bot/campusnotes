1.1 Meaning and Essentials of a Valid Contract

In simple terms, everyday life involves many promises. When you go to a shop to buy a pen, you promise to pay ₹10, and the shopkeeper promises to give you the pen. But can you go to court if the shopkeeper doesn't give you the pen? Only if that promise is a "Contract". The Indian Contract Act, 1872 is the foundation law that tells us which promises are legally binding and which are just ordinary words.

Sec 2(h) - Legal Definition of Contract

"An agreement enforceable by law is a contract."
Formula to remember: Contract = Agreement + Legal Enforceability.

Imagine you want to build a house. A contract is exactly like that house. It has pillars (essential elements). If even one pillar is missing, the house collapses, and the contract becomes invalid. As per Sec 10, there are specific "pillars" that must be present:

  • Offer & Acceptance: One person must make a clear proposal (offer), and the other person must agree to it completely. If the offer is vague, or the acceptance is partial, the contract fails.
  • Lawful Consideration: This is the "price" you pay for a promise. It can be money, goods, or even doing a favor. A promise without something in return is just a gift, not a contract.
  • Capacity to Contract: The people making the promise must be legally capable. For example, a minor or a mentally unsound person cannot make a contract because they may not understand the consequences.
  • Free Consent: Both parties must agree voluntarily, without any pressure or cheating. If one party forces the other to sign a paper, it is not free consent.
  • Lawful Object: The purpose of the agreement must be legal. You cannot make a contract to rob a bank or do something illegal.
  • Intention to Create Legal Relations: There must be a serious intent to go to court if the promise is broken. Social invitations or friendly family jokes are not contracts.
  • Certainty & Possibility: The contract cannot be based on vague terms like "I will buy your goods sometime later." It must be specific and physically possible to perform.
Pro-Tip

In exams, if a question asks for "Essentials of a valid contract", always use the same order as above and explain each point with a small example. You will easily get full marks!

1.2 Offer & Acceptance (Proposal)

Before a contract exists, there must be a "meeting of minds". This meeting starts with one person making an Offer, and the other giving an Acceptance. Let's understand this carefully, as it is the most common exam question.

Sec 2(a) - Definition of Offer/Proposal

"When one person signifies to another his willingness to do or to abstain from doing anything, with a view to obtaining the assent of that other to such act or abstinence, he is said to make a proposal."

Legal Rules for a Valid Offer:

  • Offer must be communicated: Just thinking about selling a car is not an offer. The offer must reach the other person (offeree). The landmark case Lalman Shukla v. Gauri Dutt teaches us this very well.
  • Offer must be definite: If I say, "I will sell you my car for a reasonable price," that is vague. How much is "reasonable"? It must be a fixed price or a clear term.
  • Express or Implied: Offers can be spoken (express) or shown through actions (implied). For example, getting into an auto-rickshaw is an implied offer to pay the fare.
  • Distinction from Invitation to Offer: This is extremely important! A shop displaying goods with a price tag is NOT an offer. It is an "invitation to treat/offer". You make the actual offer when you take the item to the cashier, and the shopkeeper accepts it. Similarly, catalogs, auction notices, and tenders are merely invitations, not legal offers.

Legal Rules for Valid Acceptance:

  • Mirror Rule: Acceptance must be absolute and unqualified. If I say "I will sell for ₹100," and you say "I agree, but I will pay ₹90," that is NOT acceptance. It is a counter-offer.
  • Proper Communication: Acceptance must be communicated to the offeror. Simply mentally agreeing does not make a contract.
  • Time Limits: Acceptance must be done within a reasonable time. If I offer to sell a laptop today, and you accept it a year later, I am not bound.

1.3 Consideration

Consideration is a fancy legal word that means "something in return." In everyday Hinglish, it means "Jab aap kuch maangte ho, toh kuch dena bhi padta hai". It is the price you pay to buy the promise.

Sec 2(d) - Definition of Consideration

"When at the desire of the promisor, the promisee or any other person has done or abstains from doing, or does or abstains from doing, or promises to do or to abstain from doing something, such act or abstinence or promise is called a consideration for the promise."
Core Rule: "No consideration, no contract."

Let's understand consideration with a simple example:
If X promises to sell his bike to Y for ₹10,000, then the ₹10,000 is the consideration for Y, and the bike is the consideration for X. Both parties must give something to get something. Consideration can be past (done before the promise), present (done at the time), or future (promised to be done). However, if one party gives a promise for nothing in return, the law calls it a "gift", and courts will not enforce it.

⚠️ Important: Exceptions to "No Consideration, No Contract" (Sec 25)

Like every rule, there are 5 major exceptions where a contract is valid even without consideration:

  • Natural Love & Affection: If two blood relatives (like a father and son) make a written and registered contract out of love, it is valid even without money exchanged.
  • Past Voluntary Services: If you voluntarily helped someone in an emergency, and they later promise to pay you, that promise is legally valid.
  • Time-Barred Debt: If a debt is very old and legally expired, but the debtor signs a fresh written promise to pay it, it becomes a valid contract again.
  • Agency (Sec 185): You don't need to pay a fee or give anything in return to appoint someone as your agent. It works automatically.
  • Valid Gifts: If you give a gift to someone under the Gift Deed and get it registered, the law does not ask for anything in return.

1.4 Capacity to Contract (Competency)

The law says: Who is allowed to make a contract? Not everyone! Only people who are mature enough to understand the risks can enter into a legally binding agreement. Sec 11 gives us the three Golden Rules for this.

  • Age of Majority: You must have completed 18 years of age. Anyone under 18 is a minor.
  • Sound Mind: You must be mentally fit to understand the contract and its consequences. A person who is insane or lunatic cannot contract.
  • Not Disqualified by Law: Certain people are disqualified by specific laws, such as an insolvent person (bankrupt) or a foreign sovereign.
⚖️ Landmark Case (Minor's Agreement): Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose (1903)

Facts: Dharmodas Ghose (a minor) mortgaged his property to a moneylender. When he turned 18, he refused to pay the debt, claiming he was a minor when he signed the papers.

Judgment: The Privy Council ruled that a contract with a minor is "void ab initio" (void from the very beginning). This means it is completely dead from day one. The moneylender could not recover the money at all!

What about Restitution? The court also ruled that if a minor gets property by lying about his age, and the property is still with him, the court can order him to return the property. But if he has already used it, eaten it, or sold it, he cannot be forced to give money compensation.

Important note on Minors

Sec 68 (Necessaries): While a minor cannot make contracts, if a kind person gives a minor "necessaries" (things needed for survival, like food, clothes, shelter, or medicine), the minor will have to pay for them out of their own property. This is the only exception where a minor is legally liable to pay.

1.5 Free Consent (Vitiating Factors)

Let's say you agree to buy a house, but you were forced to sign the papers at gunpoint. Would that be a valid agreement? Absolutely not! For a contract to be valid, your "Consent" must be "Free". If it is not, the contract becomes Voidable (can be cancelled) or Void (completely invalid). There are 5 factors that destroy free consent:

  • Coercion (Sec 15): This means physically forcing someone to make a contract by threatening them, or by locking them up.
    Example: If A threatens to kidnap B's child unless B signs a property deed, the contract is voidable.
  • Undue Influence (Sec 16): This happens when one person is in a position of power over the other (like a doctor over a patient, or a father over a son) and unfairly pressures the weaker person into making a contract.
    Example: A teacher forces a student to sell his land at half the price because he is scared of failing in exams. The student can cancel it later.
  • Fraud (Sec 17): Simply put, this is cheating! It means intentionally telling a lie to trick the other person into signing the contract.
    Example: A seller tells a buyer that a ring is pure gold, but it is actually copper. This is fraud.
  • Misrepresentation (Sec 18): This is also telling a lie, but the person doesn't know it is a lie. They are being innocent but careless.
    Example: A seller says, "This house is 100 years old" because they read it in a record, but the actual record was wrong. The seller didn't know it was false, but it's still misrepresentation.
  • Mistake (Sec 20-22): This means both parties have the wrong idea about something crucial.
    Example (Bilateral Mistake): A thinks they are buying a Maruti car, and B thinks they are selling a Hyundai car. They both assume the other is buying their own brand! This contract is completely VOID.

1.6 Legality of Object & Void Agreements

Even if all the pillars (offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity, free consent) are present, the contract will be thrown out of court if the "Object" (the purpose) of the agreement is illegal or against public welfare.

Sec 23 - Unlawful Objects

The object is unlawful if it is: Forbidden by law, defeats the purpose of any law, is fraudulent, causes injury to a person or property, or is opposed to public policy (like promoting prostitution or gambling).

What is a Void Agreement? (Sec 24 to 30): Void agreements are agreements that are completely useless in the eyes of the law. Let's look at the most important ones:

  • Sec 26 (Restraint of Marriage): An agreement that prevents someone from getting married is void.
  • Sec 27 (Restraint of Trade): This is a very hot topic in exams! If you make a deal to stop someone from doing their business forever, it is void.
    Exception: If you buy a shop from someone, you can legally make them sign a contract saying "Do not open a similar shop within 5 kilometers for 2 years." This is allowed because it protects your business goodwill.
  • Sec 28 (Restraint of Legal Proceedings): If you force someone to agree that they cannot go to court or can only go to a very faraway court, the agreement is void.
  • Sec 30 (Wagering Agreements): Pure gambling/betting agreements are void (except in a few cases like horse racing in some states, which are treated differently).

1.7 Quasi-Contracts

Let's be real: Sometimes, people get benefits without making any legal promise. Should they be allowed to keep the benefit for free? The law says No. Quasi-contracts (Sec 68 to 72) are obligations created by law to prevent "Unjust Enrichment" (meaning, making money unfairly).

  • Sec 68 (Necessaries to Minors): We already discussed this. If someone provides food to a minor, the minor has to pay out of his own property.
  • Sec 69 (Reimbursement): If you pay a debt that legally belongs to someone else (to protect your own property), that person must pay you back.
  • Sec 70 (Benefit of a Non-Gratuitous Act): If you accidentally do a good deed for someone, and they accept the benefit, they are bound to pay for it. For example, a neighbor gets a new water tank installed by mistake, but they start using it. They must pay the plumber.
  • Sec 71 (Finder of Goods): If you find a lost watch on the road, you cannot just keep it. You have a duty to return it to the owner. The finder is considered a bailee (custodian).
  • Sec 72 (Money Paid by Mistake): If you pay extra money to someone by mistake (e.g., bank error), they must return it. They cannot keep it just because you were careless.

📝 Exam Prep & PYQ Analysis

Now that you have understood the concepts, let's see how to write perfect answers for the questions that come directly from this unit in your Semester exams. These are structured for the standard 6+6+6 marks format.

PYQ Analysis: Question 1 (6+6+6 Marks)

Q(a) Define the term "void agreements"? Discuss the agreements in restraint of trade with suitable examples.

Exam Answer Structure:

  • Definition (1 Mark): As per Section 2(g) of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, a "Void Agreement" is one that is not enforceable by law. Such agreements have no legal effect whatsoever.
  • Void vs Illegal (1 Mark): Be careful! A void agreement is just unenforceable. However, an illegal agreement is not only unenforceable but also considered a criminal offense, punishable by law (e.g., smuggling).
  • Restraint of Trade (3 Marks): Section 27 clearly says that any agreement that stops a person from practicing their lawful trade, profession, or business is void. However, there are 2 major exceptions:
    • Sale of Goodwill: If you sell your business and its goodwill, you can agree to not start a similar business within a specific local area.
    • Partnership Acts: Partners can agree that they will not compete with their own partnership firm while they are partners.
  • Examples (1 Mark):
    • Void Example: A doctor signs a contract promising never to practice medicine anywhere in the entire country. This is too broad, hence void.
    • Valid Example: A shopkeeper sells his grocery store and agrees not to open another similar store within 5 km of his old shop for 2 years. This is valid.

Q(b) Define the term "offer". What are the legal rules relating to a valid offer?

Exam Answer Structure:

  • Definition (1 Mark): Under Section 2(a), when a person clearly shows their willingness to do or not do something to get the agreement of the other party, it is called a valid Offer.
  • Legal Rules for a Valid Offer (4 Marks):
    • The offer must be communicated to the person to whom it is made (Lalman Shukla v. Gauri Dutt).
    • The terms of the offer must be clear, definite, and not vague.
    • The offer must be made with the serious intention to create a legal obligation.
    • It can be in any form: express (spoken/written) or implied (actions).
    • A student must remember the difference between an offer and an "invitation to offer". A price list in a shop is a mere invitation to the customer to make an offer.
  • Conclusion (1 Mark): Without a valid offer, there is no foundation for a contract. The offer is the very first building block.

Q(c) "An agreement with a minor is void ab initio." Discuss. Also state provisions related to restitution.

Exam Answer Structure:

  • Basic Law (2 Marks): As per Section 11, a person below the age of 18 (a minor) does not have the legal capacity to make a contract. The leading case of Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose established that any contract made by a minor is void ab initio (dead from the very start).
  • Results of a Minor's Contract (2 Marks):
    • The minor cannot be held liable for any breach of promise.
    • The minor cannot be asked to pay damages.
    • The contract cannot be later ratified (approved) even after the minor turns 18.
    • A minor CAN, however, be a partner to share profits, but cannot be held liable for losses.
  • Restitution (2 Marks): Restitution means "giving back". If a minor lies about his age and gets property or goods, and that property/goods are still physically in his possession, the court can order the minor to return them. However, if the goods have been eaten, destroyed, or used up, the court cannot force the minor to pay money for them.
