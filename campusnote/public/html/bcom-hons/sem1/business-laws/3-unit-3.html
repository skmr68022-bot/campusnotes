3.1 Contract of Indemnity

In day-to-day life, we often hear about "insurance". Whether it's car insurance, medical insurance, or travel insurance, the underlying idea is simple: someone promises to cover our financial losses if something bad happens. In legal terms, this is called the Contract of Indemnity. To put it simply, if you agree to protect someone against a loss that might happen, you are entering into a contract of indemnity.

It's essential to understand that a contract of indemnity is a primary liability. This means that as soon as the loss occurs, the indemnifier (the person promising to pay) must immediately compensate the indemnity-holder. They cannot wait to see if the loss gets worse or try to shift the blame. This contract is usually created by an express promise in writing, but in some cases, it can be implied by the circumstances of the transaction as well.

Sec 124 - Legal Definition

A contract by which one party promises to save the other from loss caused to him by the conduct of the promisor himself, or by the conduct of any other person, is called a "Contract of Indemnity".

Who are the parties? Unlike other contracts, this one involves only 2 parties:

  • Indemnifier: The person who makes the promise to compensate for the loss.
  • Indemnity-holder: The person who is being protected against the loss.

Rights and Duties of the Indemnity-holder (Sec 125)

When the indemnity-holder faces a lawsuit or loses some property covered by the contract, they have specific legal rights. Under Section 125, they can recover three things from the indemnifier: (1) All the damages they are forced to pay in a lawsuit, (2) The legal costs (lawyer's fees) they incurred while defending themselves, and (3) The expenses they paid to settle the case, provided these expenses were reasonable.

On the flip side, what about the rights of the Indemnifier? While not explicitly mentioned in the act, the law implies that when the indemnifier pays the loss, they are "subrogated" to the rights of the indemnity-holder. This means the indemnifier can step into the shoes of the indemnity-holder and sue the actual wrongdoer to recover their money.

🔍 Important Distinction

Indemnity vs. Insurance: Many students confuse these two. While all insurance contracts are contracts of indemnity, not all contracts of indemnity are insurance. Insurance requires a specific statutory policy (like a motor policy) and an insurable interest, whereas a general contract of indemnity can be made between ordinary people for any lawful purpose.

3.2 Contract of Guarantee

Let's imagine a practical scenario: Your best friend wants to buy a flat worth ₹50 Lakhs. He takes a loan from a bank. But the bank manager does not fully trust his salary to repay the loan. So, the manager says, "We will give the loan only if you (the friend) find a wealthy relative who can co-sign and guarantee the loan." When that relative signs the papers, they are entering into a Contract of Guarantee.

The key distinction from Indemnity is that a Contract of Guarantee involves 3 distinct parties. The liability of the Surety is "secondary". In plain English, the bank is supposed to chase your friend for the loan first. Only if your friend defaults completely will the bank come to you (the Surety) to pay.

Sec 126 - Legal Definition

A contract to perform the promise, or discharge the liability, of a third person in case of his default. The person who gives the guarantee is called the 'Surety'; the person for whom the guarantee is given is the 'Principal Debtor'; and the person to whom the guarantee is given is the 'Creditor'.

The 3 Parties:

  • Principal Debtor: The person who actually takes the loan/benefit.
  • Creditor: The person who provides the loan/benefit.
  • Surety: The person who signs the guarantee promising to pay if the debtor defaults.

Nature of Surety's Liability and Discharge

Under Section 128, the liability of the Surety is "co-extensive" with that of the Principal Debtor. This is a fancy way of saying the Surety is bound to pay the exact same debt amount, interest, and costs as the debtor. However, the law also protects the Surety in certain situations. The Surety is discharged from their duty under Sections 133 to 144 if: (1) The Creditor makes any significant changes to the terms of the contract without the Surety's consent, (2) The Creditor releases the Principal Debtor from the debt, or (3) The Creditor loses or impairs any security that the Surety had provided earlier.

Comparison Table: Indemnity vs Guarantee

(This exact table is highly recommended for a 6-mark short-note question in exams.)

Basis of Difference Contract of Indemnity Contract of Guarantee
Number of Parties Only 2 parties (Indemnifier and Indemnity-holder). 3 parties (Principal Debtor, Creditor, Surety).
Number of Contracts Only 1 contract between the 2 parties. 3 separate contracts: Debtor-Creditor, Debtor-Surety, Surety-Creditor.
Nature of Liability Primary Liability: The indemnifier is immediately liable when the loss occurs. Secondary Liability: The surety is only liable if the principal debtor defaults.

3.3 Contract of Bailment

Have you ever gone to a library and handed a book to a librarian, or left your clothes at a dry cleaner, or parked your car in a paid parking lot? In all these situations, you are handing over your physical goods to someone else for a temporary purpose. You are giving them "possession", but you still own the goods. In legal terms, this is called Bailment. The word "Bailment" comes from the French word "Bailler", which means to deliver.

The concept of Bailment can be categorized into 3 types depending on who benefits from it: (1) For the sole benefit of the Bailor (e.g., you leave your jewelry with a friend for free safekeeping). (2) For the sole benefit of the Bailee (e.g., you lend your bicycle to a neighbor for free). (3) For mutual benefit (e.g., you rent a safe deposit locker at a bank, where both you and the bank benefit). Understanding this classification helps courts decide how much care the Bailee must take.

Sec 148 - Legal Definition

The delivery of goods by one person to another for some purpose, upon a contract that they shall, when the purpose is accomplished, be returned or otherwise disposed of according to the directions of the person delivering them.

Bailor: The person delivering the goods (You).
Bailee: The person receiving the goods (Librarian, Dry cleaner, Garage owner).

Duties of the Bailee and Rights of the Bailor

Under Section 151, the Bailee must take as much care of the goods as a reasonable, prudent person would take of their own goods under similar circumstances. If the Bailee is negligent, they are liable for the loss. For example, if you give a ₹50,000 watch to a jeweler for repair, and the jeweler carelessly keeps it on the edge of the counter where it falls and breaks, they will have to pay ₹50,000. However, if they take all reasonable precautions but the watch is still stolen by a professional thief, they are not liable.

On the other side, the Bailor has clear rights: The Bailor can demand the return of the goods as soon as the purpose of the bailment is complete. The Bailor can also sue the Bailee for compensation if the Bailee misuses the goods (e.g., uses the car for a business trip when it was given just for a personal errand).

Finder of Goods (Sec 71)

What does the law say if you find a lost wallet on the street? You might think "Finders keepers!"—but the law thinks differently. When you pick up the wallet, you instantly become a Bailee. Under Section 71, you are legally bound to take reasonable care of it, try to locate the true owner, and return it to them. If you keep it without trying to find the owner, you are committing a civil wrong, and the true owner can sue you to get the wallet back along with compensation for any damages.

3.4 Contract of Pledge

Let's talk about a very common financial practice in India: the "Gold Loan". When you walk into a bank or a pawnbroker and hand over your gold chain as security for a cash loan, you are making a Pledge. A Pledge is actually a very specific type of Bailment. In standard bailment, you give goods for *safekeeping or repair*. In pledge, you give goods specifically as *security* to ensure a debt is repaid.

Sec 172 - Legal Definition

The bailment of goods as security for payment of a debt or performance of a promise is called a 'Pledge'. The Bailor is called the 'Pawnor', and the Bailee is called the 'Pawnee'.

Rights of the Pawnee (Sec 173 & 176)

The Pawnee (the lender) has two extremely important rights. First, the Right of Retainer (Sec 173) - The Pawnee can keep the goods until the debt is fully repaid. This right even extends to any interest or legal costs that the debtor owes.

Second, the Right to Sell (Sec 176) - If the Pawnor (the borrower) defaults on their payment, the Pawnee cannot just immediately sell the goods for profit. They must give the Pawnor a reasonable notice stating that they will sell the goods if the loan isn't repaid. After this notice expires, the Pawnee can sell the goods at a public auction. If they get more money than the debt, they must return the surplus to the borrower. If they get less money, the borrower still owes the deficit.

Comparison Table: Pledge vs Bailment

(This is a frequent question in semester exams. Write this out clearly!)

Basis of Difference General Bailment Pledge
Primary Purpose For safekeeping, repair, carriage, or use of goods. Strictly for providing security for a loan or debt.
Right to Sell on Default The bailee cannot sell the goods if the bailor doesn't pay. The pawnee can sell the goods (after giving reasonable notice) if the pawnor defaults on the loan.

3.5 Contract of Agency

Running a successful business is a huge task. Can the CEO of a company personally sign every single purchase order, hire every single employee, and negotiate every single contract? Absolutely not. Therefore, they delegate this power to employees, managers, and brokers. In legal terms, these delegates are called Agents, and the person they represent is the Principal. The Contract of Agency is special because it doesn't require any financial consideration—you can make someone your agent just by giving them permission, without paying them a fee.

Sec 182 - Legal Definition

An 'Agent' is a person employed to do any act for another or to represent another in dealings with third persons. The person for whom such act is done, or who is so represented, is called the 'Principal'.

5 Ways an Agency is Created (Sec 185)

  • By Express Authority: The principal directly tells the agent, either orally or in writing, "Buy these shares for me." This is the most common method.
  • By Implied Authority: The principal gives the agent a specific job. The law automatically allows the agent to do *everything reasonably necessary* to complete that job. For example, if you hire a manager to run your restaurant, they are impliedly allowed to order food supplies without you checking every single potato.
  • Agency by Estoppel (Holding Out): The principal acts or behaves in a way that makes a third party reasonably believe that someone is their agent. The principal cannot later deny it. For example, a shopkeeper's wife often runs the cash register. A regular customer pays the wife, but the shopkeeper later claims she is not his agent. The court will rule against the shopkeeper because he "held her out" as his agent.
  • Agency by Necessity: This arises when an emergency occurs, the principal is unreachable, and immediate action is required to save the principal's property from destruction. For example, a ship's captain sells the cargo at a low price in a storm to pay for emergency repairs to save the ship and the remaining cargo. The captain acts as an agent of necessity, and the principal must accept this sale.
  • Agency by Ratification: This is a "Do it first, ask later" situation. An agent does a specific act without having any authority from the principal. However, the principal reviews the act later, decides it is beneficial, and publicly "ratifies" (approves) it. Legally, ratification makes the act valid retroactively, as if the agent had full authority from day one. There are strict rules: the principal must know all the facts, and ratify the entire act, not just a part of it.

Duties and Obligations of the Agent & Principal

An agent has specific duties under Sections 211 to 220. First, the agent must conduct the business according to the principal's directions (Sec 211). If they go beyond their authority, they are personally liable for any losses. Second, the agent must use reasonable skill and diligence (Sec 212). If they are negligent and cause a loss to the principal, the agent must pay. On the other hand, the principal owes duties to the agent as well: The principal must pay the agent their agreed commission or fees. If the principal hires an agent to do a task and then revokes the agency before it is completed, the agent is still legally entitled to the full commission under Section 222.

Termination of Agency

Just like a contract, an agency must end at some point. The law divides termination into two broad categories:

  • By Act of Parties: The Principal can revoke the agent's authority, the Agent can give up the job (renunciation), or the specific business for which the agent was hired is completed.
  • By Operation of Law: This is automatic and beyond the parties' control. This includes the death or insanity of the principal or agent, the bankruptcy (insolvency) of the principal, or if the subject matter of the agency is destroyed (e.g., a broker was hired to sell a house, but the house burned down).
⚡ Special Exam Focus: Irrevocable Agency

Sec 202: Can an agency become 'irrevocable' (unable to be ended) by the principal? Yes! If the agent has a personal interest in the subject matter of the agency (like a half-share in the profit), and the principal tries to revoke the agency, the law will not allow it. The agency is said to be "coupled with an interest" and becomes irrevocable.

PYQ Analysis (6+6+6 Marks)

Q(a) What is meant by 'Agency by Estoppel' and 'Agency by Necessity'? Explain the provisions of the Indian Contract Act relevant to them.

Exam Answer Structure:

  • Agency by Estoppel (2 Marks): This doctrine is also known as "Holding Out". It applies when the principal, through his words or actions, leads a third party to believe that someone else is his agent. Because the principal created this belief, the law "estops" (stops) him from later denying the agency if the third party relied on it in good faith. (Sec 182-185 supports this general principle).
  • Agency by Necessity (2 Marks): This is a common law exception applied during emergencies. If the principal's property is in immediate danger, it is impossible to contact the principal, and someone must act to save the property from destruction, that person becomes an agent of necessity. The principal is bound by the decisions of this agent.
  • Example (2 Marks): Estoppel: A shop owner lets his nephew take orders. A customer places a big order with the nephew. The owner cannot refuse the order claiming the nephew wasn't an agent.
    Necessity: A stable owner's horse is trapped in a burning barn. The neighbor breaks the lock, pulls the horse out, and feeds it. The owner must pay the neighbor because he acted as an agent of necessity to save the horse.

Q(b) Write a short note on: Contract of Indemnity v/s Contract of Guarantee.

Exam Answer Structure:

  • Opening Statement (1 Mark): Though both contracts deal with compensating someone for a loss, they are fundamentally different in their core structure and legal liability.
  • Key Differences (4 Marks): Simply copy the comparison table provided in Section 3.2 of this guide, focusing heavily on "Primary vs Secondary liability" and "2 parties vs 3 parties".
  • Conclusion (1 Mark): The easiest way to remember the difference is this: In Indemnity, A promises B "I will pay for your loss". In Guarantee, A promises C "I will pay B's debt if B fails to pay you."
