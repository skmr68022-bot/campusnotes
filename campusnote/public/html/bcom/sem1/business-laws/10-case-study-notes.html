Case Study 1: Minor's Agreement

Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose (1903)

📖 Facts: Dharmodas Ghose, a minor (under 18), mortgaged his property to a moneylender to get a loan. When he refused to pay back the money, the moneylender filed a suit against him.

⚖️ Legal Principle: The Privy Council ruled that an agreement entered into by a minor is "Void ab initio" (void from the very beginning). A minor cannot be held liable for any debt or breach of contract.

📝 Key Exam Rule - Restitution: If a minor fraudulently misrepresents their age and obtains property, they can be forced to return the property only if it still exists in their possession. However, if the goods are consumed/destroyed, the minor cannot be forced to pay monetary compensation.

🔹 Mapped Unit: Unit 1 (Capacity to Contract - Sec 11).

📌 How to use in exams

If a question asks about a minor's liability, always start your answer with: "As established in Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose, a minor's contract is void ab initio..." and then explain the exception of restitution.

Case Study 2: Remoteness of Damages

Hadley v. Baxendale (1854)

📖 Facts: The plaintiff (Hadley) owned a mill. The crankshaft of the mill broke. The defendant (Baxendale), a carrier, was hired to deliver the broken shaft to an engineer to get a new one made. Baxendale delayed the delivery. Due to the delay, the mill sat idle. Hadley sued Baxendale for the loss of profits during the idle period.

⚖️ Legal Principle - Rule of Remoteness: The court established a 2-part rule for claiming damages:

  • Ordinary Damages: Losses that naturally arise in the usual course of things. These are always claimable.
  • Special Damages: Losses caused by unusual, special circumstances. These can only be claimed if the breaching party had prior knowledge of these special circumstances at the time the contract was made.

📝 Key Exam Rule: The court denied Hadley the loss of profit because he failed to inform Baxendale about the urgency of the broken mill. The loss of profit was too "remote" to be claimed.

🔹 Mapped Unit: Unit 2 (Remedies for Breach - Damages).

Case Study 3: Anticipatory Breach

Hochster v. De La Tour (1853)

📖 Facts: De La Tour hired Hochster as a courier/messenger, with the job starting on June 1st. However, on May 28th (before the job was supposed to start), De La Tour canceled the contract. Hochster immediately filed a lawsuit against De La Tour for breach of contract.

⚖️ Legal Principle: The court allowed Hochster to sue immediately. This established the doctrine of "Anticipatory Breach" (Sec 39). The victim does not have to wait until the actual due date to sue. They can immediately accept the breach and claim damages.

🔹 Mapped Unit: Unit 2 (Breach of Contract).

📝 Direct PYQ Application

Question: Differentiate between actual and anticipatory breach of contract.
Answer Tip: Start with the definition of actual breach (on the due date), then define anticipatory breach (before the due date), and specifically cite Hochster v. De La Tour to show the victim can sue immediately.

Case Study 4: Doctrine of Frustration

Taylor v. Caldwell (1863)

📖 Facts: Taylor rented a music hall from Caldwell to hold a concert. Before the concert date, the music hall was completely destroyed by an accidental fire, with no fault of either party.

⚖️ Legal Principle - Supervening Impossibility: The court ruled that the contract was discharged by "Supervening Impossibility" (Frustration). When the specific subject matter (the hall) is destroyed without the parties' fault, the contract becomes impossible to perform and automatically ends. Both parties were freed from their obligations.

📝 Key Exam Rule: This is the foundational case for Sec 56 of the Indian Contract Act. Remember: Frustration applies only to actual destruction/impossibility, not to financial loss or unprofitability.

🔹 Mapped Unit: Unit 2 (Modes of Discharge - Impossibility).

Case Study 5: Communication of Offer

Lalman Shukla v. Gauri Dutt (1913)

📖 Facts: Gauri Dutt's nephew ran away from home. Gauri Dutt sent his servant, Lalman Shukla, to search for the nephew. While Lalman Shukla was still searching, Gauri Dutt announced a reward (Rs. 501) for anyone who found the boy. Lalman Shukla found the boy but had not heard of the reward announcement. He later claimed the reward.

⚖️ Legal Principle: The court ruled that an offer must be communicated to the offeree to be valid. Since Lalman Shukla found the boy without any knowledge of the reward, he could not accept the offer. A contract cannot be formed without the offeree knowing about the offer.

🔹 Mapped Unit: Unit 1 (Offer and Acceptance).

Case Study 6: PYQ Application - Mr. Rahim's Lost Profit

📝 DU PYQ Scenario (6 Marks)

Problem: Mr. Rahim, a renowned tailor, delivered clothes at Surat railway station for consignment to Lucknow on 1st November. He needed the lot delivered by the end of November because demand for Sherwanis was at its peak during that season. He did not inform the railways about this peak-season urgency. The consignment was delayed by 1 month. Is Mr. Rahim entitled to claim the loss of profit?

🔴 Step-by-Step Answer Structure:

  • Identify the rule: This situation is governed by the Rule of Remoteness of Damages established in Hadley v. Baxendale. Loss of profit (Special Damages) is only claimable if the breaching party had prior knowledge of the special circumstances.
  • Analyze the facts: Mr. Rahim needed the clothes urgently for the peak season, but he failed to inform the railways about this special seasonal demand at the time of dispatch.
  • State the conclusion: Because the railways were unaware of the special circumstances, Mr. Rahim cannot claim the loss of profit. The loss is considered "too remote". He can only recover ordinary damages (e.g., cost of delayed transport, storage fees).

Case Study 7: Doctrine of Nemo Dat Quod Non Habet

The "Nemo Dat" Rule (Sec 27, Sale of Goods Act)

📖 Principle: "No one can give what they do not possess." If a seller does not have legal ownership of the goods, they cannot transfer a valid title to the buyer.

⚖️ Exceptions to the rule: The buyer can still get a valid title in these 5 situations:

  • Sale by Mercantile Agent: An agent sells goods in the ordinary course of business with the owner's consent.
  • Sale by Co-owner: One co-owner sells the goods with the consent of the other co-owner.
  • Sale under Voidable Title: The seller obtained goods through fraud, but the contract hasn't been cancelled yet.
  • Sale by Unpaid Seller: An unpaid seller re-sells goods they had reclaimed.
  • Sale by Estoppel: The original owner negligently lets the seller appear to be the owner.

🔹 Mapped Unit: Unit 4 (Transfer of Ownership / Sale by Non-Owners).

Case Study 8: Doctrine of Caveat Emptor

Caveat Emptor (Sec 16, Sale of Goods Act)

📖 Principle: "Let the buyer beware." It is the buyer's duty to examine the goods and ensure they satisfy their needs. If they buy a faulty product without asking, they must suffer the loss.

⚖️ Exceptions (When the seller must pay): The Doctrine of Caveat Venditor ("Let the seller beware") applies in these 6 situations:

  • When the buyer tells the seller the specific purpose and relies on the seller's skill.
  • When the seller commits fraud (intentionally lies).
  • When the seller conceals a latent defect (hidden defect not visible on examination).
  • When there is a breach of the implied condition of merchantable quality.
  • When the goods are bought under a patent/trade name.
  • When the seller prevents the buyer from examining the goods.

🔹 Mapped Unit: Unit 4 (Conditions & Warranties).

🎯 Exam Pro-Tip: How to write Case Studies

  • The "IRAC" Method: Always structure your case study answers using Issue (identify the legal problem), Rule (state the relevant case law/Section), Application (apply the facts of the problem to the rule), and Conclusion (state the final outcome).
  • Don't overdo the facts: For landmark cases like Mohori Bibee or Hadley v. Baxendale, you only need 1-2 lines of facts. The bulk of your marks come from explaining the Legal Principle established.
  • Use case names as "Triggers": If the question mentions "Minor", immediately write Mohori Bibee. If "Loss of Profit", immediately write Hadley v. Baxendale. This signals to the examiner that you know your core cases.
